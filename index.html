Energy.gov Redesign Case Study

My colleage Ryan and I partnered up for a project and chose to redesign the energy.gov webpage. Due to the increase of environmental issues, we feel it is important that the information within this website needs to be more accessiable to all. For this case study, we did interviews, user testing of the original site, found common pain-points and main our redesign around those issues.

Research Process

Usability Tests

We created a few tasks for users to complete, then found some friends and family to test how well they could navigate the original website

Hueristic Evaluation

To further analyze the webpage, we completed a full analysis that discussed readibilty, content organization, use of space, use of imagery and much more.

Feature Prioritization

After our research, we started to compile all of our ideas and figure what changes were most important with balancing how much we can complete in the short three weeks we had to complete the case study.

"An Overwhelming Web Experience"

After all our research, we found that Energy.gov is extremely wordy and its search bar function is not operatable and does not offer filters to narrow down search results. Not all webpages are updated to Energy.gov's "new" design. Content organization is inconsistent and navigation options don't make sense. All this leading to a poor user experience for those wanting to learn more about the environment and what our government is doing about it.

Iterations

Complete Overhaul of Main Navigation

In addition to completely redesigning the main navigation bar. We added a global navigation to the header to take some unneed topics out of the main navigation and to put important links at the top for the user to easily find suchas; subscription link, news, leadership contacts, and more about the webpage itself.

Placing Priorities Front and Center

Before, the old webpage had their main priorities near the bottom of their homepage. We decided to give them a refreshed look but also to place them as the first thing the user see's when landing on their homepage. Also made it so the images did not take up the whole page to help encourage the user to scroll to see what else is beyond the fold.
